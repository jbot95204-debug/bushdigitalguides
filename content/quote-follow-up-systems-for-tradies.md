More enquiries do not help much if good quote requests are not tracked and followed up. For many trade and service businesses, a simple quote follow-up system can recover work that would otherwise disappear between the first call, site visit, quote, and next job.

Bush Digital Guides helps regional businesses connect enquiry forms, calls, quote requests, reminders, and follow-up into a practical system the owner can actually use. The goal is not a complicated sales machine. It is a clear way to know who asked, what they need, what was promised, and what should happen next.

## Where quote follow-up usually leaks

Common leaks include:

- missed calls that never become a written task
- website enquiries going to an inbox nobody checks every day
- quote requests with no suburb, job type, photos, or preferred contact method
- site visits or phone calls not logged anywhere
- quotes sent without a follow-up date
- open quotes spread across text messages, email, notebooks, and job software
- no simple way to see which leads came from Google, the website, referrals, or repeat customers

A follow-up system should make the next action obvious without adding admin that the team will ignore.

## What to track

Start with the minimum useful information:

- customer name and contact details
- suburb or job location
- service requested
- enquiry source
- date received
- quote sent date
- follow-up due date
- status: new, quoted, followed up, won, lost, or not suitable
- short notes on access, timing, photos, or site details

This does not need to be complex. A spreadsheet, job-management system, shared inbox, calendar task, or simple CRM can work if the process is clear and someone owns it.

## Follow-up timing

A useful rhythm for many businesses is:

- same day: acknowledge the enquiry if possible
- 1 business day: confirm missing details
- 2 to 4 business days after quote: follow up politely
- after completion: invite genuine feedback at an appropriate time

The wording should stay helpful and approval-based. No customer should be contacted through a system the business has not approved, and automated messages should not promise booking times, prices, emergency attendance, or technical outcomes unless the owner has set those rules.

## Connect it to the website and Google Profile

Your quote form should ask for enough detail to qualify the job without making the customer work too hard. For many tradies, location, job type, timing, photos, and preferred contact method are enough to start.

The same follow-up path should be easy to find from:

- the website homepage and service pages
- click-to-call buttons on mobile
- the [Google Business Profile](/google-business-profile-help-victor-harbor/)
- review and referral links where appropriate
- missed-call summaries if the business uses a phone intake system

If the website gets the enquiry but nobody follows up, the marketing problem has only moved further down the line.

## Practical digital safety for follow-up systems

Customer enquiry details should be treated carefully. A simple system still needs sensible boundaries:

- do not ask customers to send passwords, card or bank details, login links, private customer lists, or urgent legal/compliance matters through ordinary enquiry forms
- send enquiry summaries only to approved owner or team destinations
- keep access limited to people who need it
- document who owns the spreadsheet, CRM, inbox, form, phone system, and automations
- make it easy to disable or change messages if something is wrong
- review old staff, contractor, or agency access where possible
